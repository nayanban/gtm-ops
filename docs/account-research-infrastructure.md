# Account Research Infrastructure

This playbook describes how to structure account research so that enrichment work starts with account strategy, not contact chasing.

## Goal

Build reviewed account universes from firmographic, technology, hiring, and activity signals before spending time or credits on person discovery.

## Staged Model

1. Start with account-level patterning.
2. Compare known-fit accounts, high-quality manual targets, and active opportunities.
3. Build narrow account universes using objective filters.
4. Review the account universe.
5. Save or export only reviewed account sets.
6. Run person discovery or contact enrichment only for approved accounts.
7. Track cost, credits, and evidence used.

## Why Account-First Beats Contact-First

Contact-first workflows often feel productive because they produce names and emails quickly. They can still be slow, uneven, and expensive when the underlying account universe is too broad or poorly qualified.

Account-first research is usually cleaner because:

- fit can be reviewed before spending enrichment credits;
- filters are easier to explain and adjust;
- account universes can be saved, compared, and reused;
- contact discovery can focus on approved accounts;
- disqualifying signals can remove whole accounts before person-level work begins.

The practical rule: do not deepen people until the account deserves the work.

## Useful Account Signals

| Signal Type | Use |
| --- | --- |
| Industry or segment | Basic fit screen. |
| Employee count | Size and buying-motion fit. |
| Geography | Territory and coverage planning. |
| Technology adoption | Workflow maturity and pain indicators. |
| Hiring activity | Initiative or budget signal. |
| Account score | Prioritization input, not a final decision. |
| Similarity to known-fit accounts | Starting point for universe design. |

## Credit-Aware Enrichment

Treat contact enrichment as scarce:

- Do not enrich contacts across an unreviewed account universe.
- Do not run expensive enrichment loops when account filters are still broad.
- Prefer account-level comparison first.
- Deepen only selected high-priority accounts.
- Keep a record of why enrichment was justified.

## Research Tool Validation Pattern

Before relying on a research or enrichment tool, record what was tested and what was deliberately avoided.

| Validation Area | Question |
| --- | --- |
| Capability tested | What did the tool prove it can do with synthetic or reviewed inputs? |
| Data touched | Did the test avoid private records, live prospects, and unapproved exports? |
| Cost or credit risk | Could the action consume scarce credits, paid lookups, or rate-limited calls? |
| Actions avoided | Which writes, exports, enrichments, or messages were deliberately not run? |
| Output quality | Were results specific, source-backed, and useful for account review? |
| Safe escalation | What must be true before the tool can run on a reviewed account set? |

This keeps account research from turning into uncontrolled data collection. A tool can be promising and still not be approved for broad use.

## Review Questions

- What source accounts define the pattern?
- Which filters are objective and reviewable?
- Which filters are merely hypotheses?
- How many accounts remain after each filter?
- What evidence supports saving this universe?
- When is contact discovery justified?

## Output Shape

A useful account-research output should include:

- account name;
- domain;
- segment or industry;
- size range;
- geography;
- fit signals;
- disqualifying concerns;
- evidence source;
- recommended next action.
